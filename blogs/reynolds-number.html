Reynolds Number in Flow Analysis

The Reynolds Number (Re) is one of the most important dimensionless numbers in fluid mechanics and chemical engineering. It helps determine whether a fluid flow is laminar, transitional, or turbulent.

What is Reynolds Number?

Reynolds number represents the ratio of:

  • Inertial forces (tendency to keep moving)
  • Viscous forces (resistance to motion)

When inertial forces dominate, flow becomes chaotic → turbulent. When viscous forces dominate, flow is smooth → laminar.

Mathematical Formula

Re = (ρ × V × D) / μ

Where:

  • ρ = Density of fluid (kg/m³)
  • V = Velocity (m/s)
  • D = Diameter / characteristic length (m)
  • μ = Dynamic viscosity (Pa·s)

Flow Regimes Based on Reynolds Number

Reynolds Number Flow Type
Re < 2100 Laminar Flow
2100 ≤ Re ≤ 4000 Transitional Flow
Re > 4000 Turbulent Flow

Physical Meaning

✔ Low Reynolds number → Flow dominated by viscosity → smooth, orderly layers ✔ High Reynolds number → Flow dominated by inertia → swirling, chaotic motion

Example Calculation

Problem: Water flows through a 4 cm diameter pipe at 1.2 m/s. Density = 1000 kg/m³, viscosity = 0.001 Pa·s. Find Re.

Re = (1000 × 1.2 × 0.04) / 0.001
Re = 48,000

Since Re = 48,000 → Turbulent Flow.

Importance in Chemical Engineering

  • Determines pressure drop in pipelines
  • Helps in designing heat exchangers
  • Used in scale-up of chemical reactors
  • Important for mixing, agitation, and mass transfer
  • Decides whether laminar or turbulent flow correlations are used

Reynolds Number in Open Channels, Plates & Reactors

Reynolds number changes based on geometry:

  • Pipes: D = pipe diameter
  • Plates: D = distance from leading edge
  • Reactors: D = agitator diameter / tank dimension
  • Heat exchangers: D = hydraulic diameter

Conclusion

Reynolds number is essential for analyzing fluid flow behavior, selecting pump capacity, sizing reactors, estimating friction factor, and understanding flow regimes. It forms the foundation for fluid mechanics in chemical engineering.
